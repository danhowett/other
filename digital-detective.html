The Digital Detective
Your guide to using the internet without getting tricked by it.
Front Page · From the Editor

The Internet Is Pretty Amazing — Until It Lies to You.

We're growing up online. Our homework, our friends, our games and our group chats all live there. So we should probably know how to spot when it's trying to fool us.

Honestly? Our parents grew up looking things up in books. We have something better: an answer to almost any question, in seconds. That's incredible. But there's a catch — the internet doesn't always tell the truth, and sometimes the lies are dressed up to look exactly like the facts. A photo can be fake. A "news story" can be made up. Even a video of someone speaking can be invented by a computer.

We wrote this guide because nobody really teaches you this stuff at school yet, and we figured we'd work it out together. Inside you'll find what's brilliant about being online, the things to watch out for, a four-step trick used by actual fact-checkers, a quiz with twenty questions, and a list of websites that are genuinely useful (not the ones adults think we use).

Schoolwork · Homework Hacks

Stuck on Homework? The Internet Has Your Back.

If you're stuck on a maths problem at 9pm and your parents haven't done long division since 1995, the internet is the friend that doesn't sigh at you. Used properly, it's basically a free tutor.

  • Three different explanations of the same thing. If your textbook makes no sense, BBC Bitesize might. If Bitesize doesn't, Khan Academy might. If neither does, a YouTuber probably will.
  • Watch real history happen. You can read actual letters from World War One soldiers, look at the original Magna Carta, or hear a speech the way it was actually given. School books only show you bits.
  • Practise without anyone judging you. Apps like Duolingo and BBC Bitesize quizzes let you get things wrong in private, which is honestly the only way to learn.
  • Pause and rewind. A teacher can't repeat a sentence forty times. A YouTube video can. You can also slow it down if someone's talking too fast.
  • Group projects that actually work. Shared docs mean you don't have to email seven versions of the same essay back and forth. (We've all been there.)
Friends · Group Chats · Communities

Your Group Chat Is a Lifeline (Most of the Time).

School is good. Group chats are also good. Together, they make your social life what it is. Used well, the internet keeps you connected to people you actually like.

  • Friends who moved schools still feel close. A best mate who moved to another city is one message away. That used to mean letters.
  • You can find people who get your specific weird interest. Whether that's a niche video game, building Lego sets, drawing manga, or coding — your tribe is out there.
  • Revising together feels less awful. A study group chat at 8pm before a maths test is genuinely helpful. Misery loves company, but so does GCSE prep.
  • You can actually publish stuff. Share your art, post your code, write a blog. Real audiences read kids our age — you don't have to wait until you're "grown up."
  • Help when things are hard. If something's wrong and you can't tell a parent or teacher, sites like Childline exist. Talking to someone helps, even anonymously.
A serious word about group chats

Group chats can also be where cyberbullying lives. Mean nicknames, screenshots passed around, people deliberately left out of a chat, "jokes" that aren't actually jokes, ganging up on one person — it can go from fun to nasty very fast, and because it's on a screen it can feel less real to the people doing it. It isn't less real to the person it's happening to.

If it's happening to you, three things help: screenshot the evidence (so you can prove what was said), don't reply (it usually makes it worse), and tell a trusted adult, even if it feels embarrassing. You can also message Childline anonymously on 0800 1111 — they won't tell anyone unless you want them to.

If you see it happening to someone else, you don't have to be a hero — but you can quietly message that person to check they're okay. Sometimes that one message is what gets someone through the day.

The first time I got tricked online, I shared something fake to my whole group chat. The embarrassment is what taught me to check.

The Pitfalls

⚠ ⚠ ⚠
Caution · Wellbeing

The Bits Nobody Talks About at School.

Even when nothing online is technically lying to you, it can still mess with your head. Some of these we've felt ourselves. They're real.

  • That "just one more video" feeling. Apps are literally designed by adults whose job is to keep you scrolling. It's not your fault — but you can still set a timer and notice when an hour vanished.
  • Comparing yourself to people who edited their lives. Nobody posts the boring Tuesday or the bad photo. Their feed is a highlights reel. Yours is too. None of us are losing.
  • The bubble where everyone agrees with you. If your feed never shows you anything that disagrees with you, that's the algorithm protecting your feelings — but it's also stopping you learning anything new.
  • Stuff you posted ages ago can still come back. A message you sent in Year 6 could be screenshotted forever. Assume nothing online is properly private — even on apps that say it is.
  • People online aren't always who they say. An adult can pretend to be 12. A 12-year-old can pretend to be 16. Don't share your school, your address, or photos with anyone you haven't met for real.
Misinformation · Why We Get Tricked

Why Fake Stories Spread So Fast.

Researchers have found that false news travels around six times faster on social media than real news. Why? Because of us, basically.

  • It's made to make you feel something. Anger and shock make you want to share before your brain catches up. That's the whole trick.
  • It looks like real news. Fake sites copy the logos, the layout, even the writing style. From a phone screen, it's hard to tell.
  • Bots make small things look huge. Fake accounts can repost a story thousands of times, making one weird claim look like everyone's talking about it.
  • AI makes fakes really easy now. Anyone can generate a realistic photo, voice, or video in seconds. The technology that's fun in apps is the same technology that fools people.
  • It tells you what you wanted to hear. The hardest fakes to spot are the ones we already half-believed. That's why even smart people get caught.

The Detective's Toolkit

✦ ✦ ✦

People who fact-check news for a living don't actually read super-carefully — they read differently. They learned this trick called SIFT, and the whole thing takes about a minute. Once you know it, you'll do it without thinking.

STEP 01
S

Stop

Don't share or even decide what you think yet. Just pause. If something is making you angry or shocked, that's exactly the moment to slow down.

STEP 02
I

Investigate

Who actually wrote this? Open a new tab and search the website's name. Thirty seconds of checking who's behind a story tells you a lot.

STEP 03
F

Find Better

Don't trust just one site. Search the same story somewhere else. If only one weird site has it, that's suspicious. If the BBC and others have it too, fair enough.

STEP 04
T

Trace It

Quotes and photos get ripped out of context all the time. Right-click and reverse-image-search photos. Click through to find the actual original story, not just the headline about it.

Eight Red Flags

⚑ ⚑ ⚑

Spotting just one of these doesn't mean a story's fake. But if you spot two or more, something's probably off.

01

HEADLINE IS SHOUTING

ALL CAPS and "YOU WON'T BELIEVE WHAT HAPPENED NEXT!!!" Real news doesn't need to scream. If it does, it's trying to switch off your brain.

02

No name, no date

Real journalists put their name on their articles. If there's no author and no date, who's actually responsible for what it says? Probably nobody good.

03

The web address looks weird

"bbc-news.co" is NOT the BBC. Watch for spelling tricks, dashes, and weird endings (like .co instead of .com). They're designed to fool a quick glance.

04

Nobody else is reporting it

If something massive happened, every news site would have it. If only one random page does, that's a clue. Big news doesn't stay quiet.

05

It says exactly what you wanted to hear

This one's sneaky. The fakes that fool you most are the ones you already half-believed. Be extra suspicious when something agrees with you a bit too perfectly.

06

The picture looks too perfect

Old photos get recycled all the time. Right-click and reverse-image-search to see where else it's been. You might find it's from years ago and a totally different event.

07

Mystery "experts"

"Scientists say…" Which scientists? Where? Real experts have names you can Google. If nobody's named, nobody's really saying anything.

08

It's making you really angry

If you want to share it because you're furious — wait. Anger is the thing fake stories are designed to cause. Come back to it tomorrow. If it's still real then, it'll still matter.

Stuff That's Actually Useful

📚 📚 📚

These are real websites we use. They're free, they're for our age, and most don't even need an account. Bookmark the ones you'll actually want — for homework, for staying safe, or for checking if a story is real.

For Homework & Studying

BBC Bitesize

UK
bbc.co.uk/bitesize

The big one. Click "KS3" if you're in Years 7–9. Covers basically every subject in school with explainers, videos, and quizzes that actually match what you're learning.

Oak National Academy

UK
thenational.academy

Free online lessons made by UK teachers. Pick your year group and subject, watch a video lesson, do a quiz. Brilliant if you missed school or just want a different teacher to explain it.

Khan Academy

Free
khanacademy.org

Thousands of short videos, mostly maths and science. Good for when you're properly stuck — pause, rewind, try the practice questions until something clicks.

CrashCourse on YouTube

Free
youtube.com/user/crashcourse

Fast, funny 10-minute videos on history, science, English, and loads more. Perfect for when you need to understand something quick before tomorrow's lesson.

Wikipedia

Free
en.wikipedia.org

Don't copy from it (teachers can tell). DO use it as a starting point. The references at the bottom of each article are gold — those are the real sources you can actually cite.

Quizlet

Free
quizlet.com

Make flashcards for whatever you're revising — or use ones other people made. The "match" game makes memorising vocab way less boring than just staring at a list.

Duolingo

Free
duolingo.com

Five minutes of French (or Spanish, or German…) before bed actually adds up. The owl gets a bit much sometimes but the lessons work.

Scratch

Free
scratch.mit.edu

Drag-and-drop coding for making your own games, animations, and stories. If you've ever thought "I'd like to build a video game," this is where to start.

Checking If Something Is Real

Full Fact

UK
fullfact.org

The UK's main fact-checking charity. If a politician or news story made a big claim and you're not sure it's true, search for it here. They probably already checked.

BBC Verify

UK
bbc.co.uk/news/reality_check

The BBC team that investigates viral claims, fake photos, and dodgy statistics. Watch how they actually do it — you can copy their methods on stuff you see online.

Google Reverse Image Search

Free Tool
images.google.com

Click the camera icon, drop in any image, and Google will tell you everywhere else on the internet that picture appears. Brilliant for catching photos that have been recycled from old events.

Snopes

Free
snopes.com

The original "is this true?" website. If your group chat is freaking out about some viral story, type it into Snopes first. They've probably already looked into it.

TinEye

Free Tool
tineye.com

Another reverse image search, but this one shows you the OLDEST version of a photo it can find. Great for proving when an "exclusive new photo" actually first appeared in 2014.

BBC Bitesize: Fake News

UK
bbc.co.uk/bitesize

BBC Bitesize has its own section on spotting fake news, made specifically for our age. If something in this guide didn't quite click, theirs explains it differently.

If Something Online Goes Wrong

Childline

UK
childline.org.uk · 0800 1111

Free, anonymous, and the call doesn't show up on the phone bill. You can talk about literally anything — bullying, family, online stuff, feeling rubbish — and they'll listen without judging.

Thinkuknow (CEOP)

UK
thinkuknow.co.uk/11_13

Run by the police (specifically the National Crime Agency). Has its own section just for our age group. If something feels wrong online — like an adult acting weird towards you — this is the place.

Childnet

UK
childnet.com

Loads of practical advice about group chats, gaming, AI, and social media — written FOR us, not at us. They actually run a film competition each year if you fancy entering.

The Mix

UK
themix.org.uk

For under-25s, so it'll grow with you. Free helpline, crisis text line, and articles on everything from money to mental health to friendships — all in normal language.

Report Harmful Content

UK
reportharmfulcontent.com

If you've reported something on a platform (Instagram, TikTok, etc.) and they didn't do anything, this UK service can help escalate it. Specifically for under-18s.

YoungMinds

UK
youngminds.org.uk

UK mental health charity for young people. Articles on stress, anxiety, exam pressure, and what to do when you feel overwhelmed. They also have a crisis text line: text YM to 85258.

Bullying UK

UK
bullying.co.uk

If you're being bullied — at school, online, anywhere — this site has actual practical advice for what to do, plus a free helpline (0808 800 2222).

Report Remove (IWF + Childline)

UK
iwf.org.uk/report-remove

If a private photo of you ever ended up online, this free service can get it taken down — confidentially. Run by Childline and the Internet Watch Foundation.

Most of these don't need accounts. If you do want to sign up for any, ask a parent or carer first — they should know which sites you use anyway. None of these are dodgy.
